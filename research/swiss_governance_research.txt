SWISS GOVERNANCE RESEARCH NOTES
================================

THREE LEVELS OF GOVERNMENT:
1. Communes (currently ~2,100) - smallest political units, closest to people
2. Cantons (26) - enjoy significant autonomy, own constitutions, own budgets
3. Confederation (federal government) - limited to powers in Federal Constitution

PRINCIPLE OF SUBSIDIARITY (Article 5a Federal Constitution):
- Nothing that can be done at a lower level should be done at a higher level
- Powers delegated UPWARD only when necessary
- Communes get maximum autonomy first
- If commune can't handle it, canton steps in
- Federal government only handles what cantons cannot

COMMUNE POWERS (village level):
- Education and schools
- Social welfare
- Energy provision
- Infrastructure (roads, property, land)
- Spatial planning
- Taxation
- Maintaining register of resident population
- Civil protection

CANTONAL POWERS:
- Budget
- Political system
- Taxation (can levy own taxes)
- All tasks not explicitly given to federal government
- Own constitutions
- Intercantonal conferences for shared issues

FEDERAL POWERS (limited to):
- Foreign policy
- National security policy
- Customs
- Currency
- Federal legislation
- National defence

DIRECT DEMOCRACY INSTRUMENTS:
1. Popular Initiative - citizens can propose constitutional amendments (100,000 signatures needed)
2. Optional Referendum - citizens can challenge laws passed by parliament (50,000 signatures)
3. Mandatory Referendum - certain decisions automatically go to popular vote

KEY FEATURES:
- Federal Council = 7 members, govern as EQUALS, decisions by CONSENSUS
- Collegial body - no single president with executive power
- Constitution regularly amended through direct democracy (popular initiative)
- Landsgemeinde (People's Assembly) still exists in 2 cantons - show of hands voting
- 3-4 popular votes per year
- ~5.5 million eligible voters

COMMUNE ASSEMBLY (GEMEINDEVERSAMMLUNG) DETAILS:
================================================
Source: SwissInfo.ch - "Swiss municipal assemblies: democracy on your doorstep" (Aug 2025)

- Over 1,650 municipalities use citizen assemblies instead of elected local parliaments
- Citizens gather in community halls or school gymnasiums
- Decisions by raising hands (or secret ballot if requested)
- Invitations dropped into letterboxes twice a year with agenda and budget
- "Assembly democracy is generally regarded as not only the oldest, but also the most directly democratic way to organise a community" - Philippe Rochat
- Key feature: space for DELIBERATION - people can speak out with doubts, not just opinions
- "Culture in which differing views can be voiced without fear of consequences"
- Participants often act with "morality of the common good" - voting on behalf of those absent
- Decisions broadly accepted despite low participation
- Some municipalities require referendum confirmation once spending surpasses threshold
- Parallels with US town meetings (Vermont) - "People tell each other what they think, but no animosity. Shake hands when they leave."
