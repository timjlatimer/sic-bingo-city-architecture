## The Central Question

The Bingo City architecture has five floors: Infrastructure, Data, Operations, Community, and Governance. The question is: **where does Marketing live?**

Three options are on the table:

1. **F6 — A new sixth floor dedicated to Marketing**
2. **Distributed across all five floors** — Marketing is present in every floor, coordinated by a Marketing avatar on the Rooftop
3. **Embedded in F4 (Community)** — Marketing is a subset of community-building

This document presents original research on how comparable organizations structure marketing, then makes a specific recommendation for the HB1000 architecture.

---

## Research Finding 1: Chime's Playbook (The Closest Comparable)

Chime is the closest real-world analog to the Ruby Red Maven ecosystem. It serves the same population (households earning under $100k, many living paycheck-to-paycheck), it is digital-first, and it grew to 8.6 million active users by solving the same core problem: *it is expensive to be poor.*

**What Chime got right about marketing structure:**

Chime did NOT have a separate "Marketing Department" that operated independently of the product. Instead, marketing was **baked into the product itself**. The referral flywheel was a product feature. The "Get paid 2 days early" message was a product truth. The SpotMe overdraft protection was both a product and a marketing story.

> *"Chime's incentive is aligned with the member: they win when people swipe, not when people slip."*

**The key insight:** When the product genuinely solves a real pain point, marketing is not a separate function — it is the act of communicating a truth people want to hear. Chime's marketing team did not create the message; the product created the message, and the marketing team amplified it.

**Channels that worked for Chime (in order of effectiveness):**
1. **Member referrals** — the single biggest acquisition channel since 2022. Cash incentives + positive product experience = viral growth.
2. **Social media community building** — authentic voice, user stories, financial tips, humor.
3. **Influencer partnerships** — creators whose audiences overlap with Ruby Red's demographic.
4. **Content marketing** — financial literacy content that ranks well in search.
5. **Integrated campaigns** — only at scale, after the core channels were proven.

**Customer acquisition cost:** ~$100 per user (vs. $600+ for traditional banks). The difference is almost entirely explained by word-of-mouth and referrals.

## Research Finding 2: CDFI Marketing (The Institutional Analog)

Community Development Financial Institutions (CDFIs) are the institutional analog to CashCo/Pawn Princess — mission-driven financial organizations serving the working poor. The CDFI Fund's own marketing guidance reveals a consistent pattern:

**How CDFIs structure marketing:**
- Marketing is NOT a standalone department in most CDFIs
- It is embedded in **community outreach** (the equivalent of F4 Community)
- The most effective channel is **trusted intermediaries** — churches, community centers, social workers, schools
- Word-of-mouth through trusted community networks outperforms any paid channel by a factor of 5–10x

**The key insight:** Ruby Red does not trust advertising. She has been burned too many times by "too good to be true" offers. The only marketing that works for her is **a recommendation from someone she already trusts**. This means the marketing function must be deeply embedded in the Community floor (F4), not in a separate Marketing floor.

## Research Finding 3: Platform Cooperative Marketing (The Structural Analog)

Platform cooperatives (like Up&Go, Stocksy, and Resonate) are community-owned platforms that serve their members rather than extracting value from them. Their marketing structure is instructive:

**How platform cooperatives structure marketing:**
- Marketing is **distributed** across the organization, not centralized
- Every member is a potential marketer (because they own the platform)
- The "marketing team" is small and focused on **enabling member advocacy**, not creating campaigns
- Content is created by members, for members, and shared by members

**The key insight:** In a cooperative model, the distinction between "user" and "marketer" dissolves. Ruby Red is not just a beneficiary of the Maven ecosystem — she is a potential advocate for it. The Peer Network (F4, position 19) is the mechanism for this. When Ruby Red tells another mom about the Emergency Fund Builder, that is the most powerful marketing the system can do.

## Research Finding 4: Social Impact Marketing Team Structure

Research from Cosmic Design and Acumen Academy on social impact marketing teams reveals a consistent pattern for organizations serving marginalized communities:

**The seven pillars of a successful social impact marketing team:**
1. **Mission alignment** — every marketing decision is filtered through "does this serve our beneficiary?"
2. **Community voice** — the community being served has a seat at the marketing table
3. **Storytelling over selling** — impact stories, not product features
4. **Trust-building over conversion** — long-term relationship, not short-term acquisition
5. **Channel humility** — go where Ruby Red already is, don't ask her to come to you
6. **Data transparency** — show her the impact, don't just claim it
7. **Peer amplification** — design for word-of-mouth from day one

**The key insight:** Social impact marketing is not a function that can be separated from the mission. It IS the mission, communicated. This argues strongly against a standalone Marketing floor.

## The Recommendation: Distributed Marketing with a Rooftop Coordinator

Based on this research, the recommendation is clear:

**Marketing should NOT be F6.** Here is why:

1. **It would create a silo.** A standalone Marketing floor would separate the marketing function from the product, community, and data functions that make marketing effective. Chime's success came from marketing being inseparable from product.

2. **It would signal the wrong priority.** For Ruby Red, a building with a "Marketing Floor" would feel like she is being sold to, not served. The architecture should feel like it is working for her, not promoting itself.

3. **It would miss the distributed nature of marketing for this population.** Ruby Red's trust is earned through every touchpoint — the product working, the community connecting her, the data being transparent, the governance protecting her. Marketing is not one floor; it is the quality of every floor.

**Instead, marketing should be distributed across all five floors, with a Marketing Coordinator avatar on the Rooftop Society.**

## The Marketing Coordinator Avatar

The Rooftop Society should add an **eighth avatar** (or repurpose an existing one) as the Marketing Coordinator. This avatar:

- Sits in the Inner Ring (inside the stanchion)
- Coordinates marketing activity across all five floors
- Ensures the brand story is consistent across all touchpoints
- Manages the referral flywheel (the Peer Network on F4)
- Reports to the Project Manager

**Proposed avatar:** The Storyteller 📖  
**Animal:** Fox — clever, adaptive, knows how to communicate across contexts  
**Role:** "The one who tells Ruby Red's story to the world — and the world's story to Ruby Red."

## The Three Marketing Channels for Ruby Red

Based on the research, the three channels that will work for Ruby Red (in order of priority):

### Channel 1: Peer Referral (The Flywheel)

**How it works:** Ruby Red tells another mom about the Emergency Fund Builder. That mom joins. Both get a small reward. The flywheel spins.

**Why it works:** 88% of consumers trust recommendations from people they know above all other forms of marketing. For Ruby Red specifically, who has been burned by advertising, a friend's recommendation is the only marketing that works.

**Implementation in Bingo City:** The Peer Network square (F4, position 19) is the mechanism. When it is built, it should include a referral system that rewards Ruby Red for bringing her community into the city.

**Chime benchmark:** Member referrals became the single biggest acquisition channel after 2022. Customer acquisition cost dropped to ~$100 vs. $600+ for traditional channels.

---

### Channel 2: Impact Storytelling (The Proof)

**How it works:** Ruby Red sees her own progress — the Emergency Fund growing, the bills getting paid on time, the deal she found at the grocery store. She shares that story. Others see it and want it.

**Why it works:** For a population that has been promised things and let down, proof is more powerful than promise. Showing Ruby Red her own progress (the Data floor) gives her a story worth telling.

**Implementation in Bingo City:** The Transparency Report (F5, position 23) and the Feedback Loop (F5, position 22) are the mechanisms. When built, they should generate shareable "progress cards" — a visual summary of what her team has accomplished for her this month.

**Research basis:** Chime's most effective content was user success stories, not product features. "I fixed my credit score using Chime" outperformed "Chime has no fees" in engagement.

---

### Channel 3: Trusted Intermediaries (The Bridge)

**How it works:** A social worker, a church, a community center, a school counselor — someone Ruby Red already trusts — introduces her to the Maven ecosystem.

**Why it works:** CDFIs consistently find that trusted intermediaries outperform direct marketing by 5–10x for the working poor. Ruby Red does not go looking for financial tools. She is introduced to them by people she already trusts.

**Implementation in Bingo City:** The Community Connect square (F4, position 17) and the Advocate square (F4, position 16) are the mechanisms. The Maven ecosystem should have a partnership program for trusted community organizations — churches, food banks, community centers, school counselors.

**Research basis:** CDFI Fund marketing guidance explicitly identifies trusted intermediaries as the primary acquisition channel for underserved populations.

## The Marketing Bingo Card (Future Initiative)

As the Maven ecosystem matures, a dedicated Marketing Bingo Card could be created — a seventh building in the city focused entirely on growth and community building. This would be the right time to create a Marketing building, not a Marketing floor.

**Proposed building:** The Town Crier 📣  
**North Star:** "Every Ruby Red knows she has a team"  
**Purpose:** Ensure every woman in Ruby Red's situation knows the Maven ecosystem exists and is working for her.

**This is the right architecture:** Marketing as its own building (initiative), not as a floor within the Maven building. The Maven building is for serving Ruby Red. The Town Crier building is for finding Ruby Red.

## Summary Recommendation

| Option | Verdict | Reason |
|--------|---------|--------|
| F6 — Marketing Floor | **Reject** | Creates silo, signals wrong priority, misses distributed nature |
| Embedded in F4 | **Partial** | F4 is the primary marketing engine, but marketing is in all floors |
| Distributed across all floors + Rooftop Coordinator | **Adopt** | Matches Chime's playbook, CDFI best practice, and cooperative model |
| Future Marketing Building (Town Crier) | **Defer** | Right idea, wrong time — build it when Maven is at 60%+ completion |
